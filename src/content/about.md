I'm a full stack developer and tech lead working primarily on AI systems. Right now I'm at RTS Labs building production AI for enterprise clients across logistics, manufacturing, and finance.

Before that I was at NimbleBrain, a small startup that hired me to do UI and quickly found out I could own the backend too. I've been shipping full stack since.

What drives me is wanting to know how things actually work. When I write TypeScript, I want to be able to trace a blocking I/O call all the way down: through libuv, into the kernel, through the container's namespace, out to the disk controller, and back up through a callback the event loop picks up on its next tick. A lot of developers writing Node day to day don't reach for that layer. I do, and I think caring about it is what separates engineers who understand their systems from engineers who only understand their code.

That instinct is why I gravitate toward infrastructure work, and it's why my side projects are starting to run that direction.

Some of them start as me solving a problem I actually have (both of my Magic: The Gathering apps came from that) and run toward being small businesses. Others exist because I want to understand something more deeply and the only honest way to do that is to build it. Either way, the project list is where most of my hours outside work go.

I'm looking for AI infrastructure roles, or AI engineering roles at a product company where the bar is high. Willing to relocate anywhere for the right role, also open to remote.

Long-term I want to go deep on Rust. I'm currently focused on Go (Golem is the vehicle for that), and once I'm where I want to be there, Rust is next.

I got into tech through music. I was a music producer first, spending long sessions building tracks in Ableton, and it turns out that building software and building a song are the same kind of work: you compose something out of many small pieces, iterate on it until it feels right, and ship it. I still make music. It's still the same instinct.
